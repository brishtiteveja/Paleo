Data and Methods

Paleo-Climate Data. The latest published records of the temperature trajectory from A.D. 200 - 1980 (1) have been adopted for this research. This includes the temperature swings of the Northern Hemisphere (NH), Southern Hemisphere (SH), and a global average, which closely match other independent research results. Such congruence of data acquired independently by different authorities suggests a high degree of accuracy with reference to both temperature and timing. These records were reconstructed by using multiproxy data, which were obtained from tree ring, coral, ice-core, borehole, and historical document studies. These recent published global and hemispheric temperature series in decadal-centennial scale are: Jones and Mann's (5) global and hemispheric temperature, A.D. 200 - 1995; Mann et al.'s (6) global temperature (PCA: tree rings, ice cores, corals), A.D.1400 - 1980; Briffa et al.'s (7) NH and regional temperature (age band decomposition: tree rings), A.D.1400 - 1995; Crowley and Lowery's (8) NH temperature (tree rings, ice cores, historical), A.D.1000 - 1993; D'arrigo et al.'s (9) NH temperature (RCS and STD: tree rings), A.D. 713 - 1995; Esper et al.'s (10) NH temperature (RCS: tree rings), A.D. 831 - 1992; Huang's (11) NH temperature (boreholes, tree ring, ice cores, corals), A.D.1500 - 1980; Jones et al.'s (12) hemispheric temperature, A.D.1000 - 1991; Mann and Jones' (1) hemispheric temperature (historical, tree rings, ice cores, sediment), A.D. 200 - 1980; Osborn and Briffa's (4) NH temperature, warm/cold spatial extent, A.D. 800 - 1995; and Rutherford et al.'s (7) NH temperature, A.D.1400 - 1971.

The above paleo-climate reconstructions can be downloaded at the web page of the World Climate Centre for Paleoclimatology (www.ncdc.noaa.gov/paleo/recons.html). We have compared these temperature series and found that the hemispheric temperature series of Mann and Jones' research paper (1) is the most comprehensive temperature data series so far in terms of its coverage and number of data sources.

The Little Ice Age. The LIA was a very important climatic event during recent human history that has been well recorded by both documentary and paleo-climate reconstructions. Research in the past 30 years has led to publication of a number of paleo-climatic time series that were reconstructed from multiproxy data. These reconstructions have shown that a warm period (Medieval Warm Period, MWP), the warmest century (20th), and a cold period (the LIA) existed globally during the last millennium (10, 13). The LIA has been dated as the period A.D.1400-1900, but A.D. 1550-1990 encompasses most published dates (12, 14). All of the studies have identified a mild period in the 18th century. Generally the LIA consists of two cold phases (16th and 17th centuries, and the 19th century), separated by a milder 18th century (13). Research in the last 10 years, in particular, has led to a significant breakthrough in high-resolution paleo-climatic reconstructions, using multiproxy data networks to reconstruct past climate variations, with a focus on the climate changes of the last millennium. These high-resolution records closely match each other in terms of terms of timing and amplitude of temperature change. Therefore, in the last few years there has been no argument about the starting and ending years of the LIA and MWP in the paleo-temperature research literature. These collected and refined paleo-climatic records have provided a strong base for analyzing the relationship between climatic change and wars.

War Data. Global war data. We used three of the most representative worldwide warfare series. Wright's (15) dataset includes all hostilities involving members of the family of nations, whether international, civil, colonial, or imperial, which were recognized as states of war in the legal sense or which involved over 50,000 troops. Luard (16) documented those encounters that involved at least one sovereign state and involved substantial, organized fighting over a significant period (principle wars). The most inclusive global war series so far is the conflict catalog compiled by Brecke*, who documents a total of 2,912 wars fought in A.D. 1400-1900, which includes all recorded violent conflicts that meet Richardson's magnitude 1.5 or higher criterion (32 or more deaths)*. The geographical locations of wars in the dataset are divided by their natural locations (SI Fig. 4). These data sets represent three important war classifications based on the magnitude, importance, and inclusiveness of war.

Brecke's conflict catalog* is an attempt to compile a data set of all recorded violent conflicts since A.D.1400. It is a superset of all other conflict compilations, being the product, the distillation of the conflicts found in over 100 different books, articles, and on-line data sets that contain listings of wars. The books include encyclopedias or handbooks of military history, compilations of major military battles, academic treatises, historical atlases, and historical chronologies of particular countries or regions. The catalog makes use of data sources in a number of languages other than English (French, German, Russian, Turkish, Arabic, Chinese, and Japanese). The ultimate goal of the catalog is to provide the empirical foundation for the construction of a taxonomy of violent conflicts analogous to what systematicists have done in zoology and botany, for example. To that end the catalog contains several variables that characterize and distinguish the different conflicts that have been identified. Significant effort has gone into resolving inconsistencies between different sources with respect to wars where they overlap.

The conflict catalog improves upon extant war data sets in a number of dimensions. For the purposes of this study, the most important is its comprehensiveness, both geographically and in terms of the types of violent conflicts encompassed. When the theoretical argument is that unfavorable climatic conditions lead to social difficulties and ultimately warfare, use of data sets that are limited in the wars they include may result in finding no causal connections. The hope is that a larger sample will improve our ability to discern a connection. Construction of the conflict catalog started from Luard's compilation (16); all of the conflicts listed in his book were entered into a computer database. Other compilations such as Wright's (15) were then examined to see whether they provided additional information. Surprisingly, the numerous compilations of wars exhibit only limited overlap in terms of the wars they include. That discovery led to the effort to assemble a comprehensive data set.

Chinese war data. China has had a very long history of civilization. There is voluminous documentation in the palaces of different dynasties, which systematically records all major events in China. This valuable documentary compilation provides a rich database for the study of wars. A group of military scientists from the Taiwan University of the Three Armies edited a book about the history of Chinese dynastic wars (18), which includes 800 wars. Krus et al. (19) also compiled a time-series of Chinese wars for the period 206 B.C. - A.D. 1911. The data sources for their study consisted of various historical books that unfortunately lacked any classification of wars. Lee (20) compiled a large number of wars for analyzing the cyclic pattern of wars as early as A.D.1933. However, he did not mention the source or sources for his study. Another group of military scientists from the Nanjing Academy of Military Sciences compiled a multivolume book that records information about wars that took place in China from 800 B.C. to A.D.1911 (2). The book includes an appendix that describe detailed information about each war, including its date, participants, location, causes, and in most cases, number of solders engaged, casualties execution of the war, and the results. A total of 1,672 wars listed in the book from A.D.1000 to 1911 have been used as the database for this study. This collection is believed to be the most comprehensive existing documentation warfare in China in terms of number of wars encompassed, data source coverage, and description of the wars, when compared with the data sources from Lee (20), Krus et al. (19), and the Taiwan University of the Three Armies (18). However, the cyclic patterns of war frequencies are the same, possibly because they share many basic data sources.

Population Data. Population data at different geographical scales were extracted from McEvedy and Jones (3). They provide figures for the population of each country through historical time. There are six parts: Europe, Asia, Africa, the Americas, Oceania, and a global overview. Each of the first five sections has a general review, and then its countries are taken in turn, with a general account of demographic progress illustrated with graphs and maps, a discussion of primary sources for population data, and a bibliography. This is a remarkably accurate work, which has been used by other scholars repeatedly. Pertaining to the population data of China, it is supplemented by Jiang's population estimates (21). As the population data are at irregular time intervals, the common logarithm of the data points was taken, linearly interpolated and then anti-logged back, to create an annual time series. This method avoids any distortions of the population growth rate resulting from the data interpolation process.

Grain Price Data (A Proxy for Food Supply Per Capita). Food supply per capita is represented by historical cereal prices because it is determined by supply (agricultural production) and demand (population size). However, only the cereal price data for Europe and China are long enough time series for our data analysis. The cereal price for Europe was obtained from Beveridge's research paper (22). The data represent the yearly fluctuation of wheat prices in Western and Central Europe from A.D. 1500 to 1869. They are based on lists of prices for long periods of consecutive years at nearly 50 separate places in England, Scotland, Holland, Flanders, France, North Germany, South Germany, Alsace, Bohemia, and Austria, as well as on official averages for whole countries, when these become available. The cereal price for China was obtained from ref. 23. The data present the decadal fluctuation of rice price for the whole of China from A.D. 1360-1909. As the rice price data are in 10-year units, the common logarithm of the data points was taken, linearly interpolated, and then anti-logged back, to create an annual time series.

Agricultural Production Data. It is a perceived wisdom that a cold climate would lead to harvest failure, especially in the past. Although the data on agricultural production for many parts of the world are unavailable in our study's time span, we could use two parameters to represent agricultural production in our quantitative analysis. The first one is the grain yield ratio in relation to seed for Europe, which was derived from ref. 24. The data set contains the grain yield ratio of the following macro regions in Europe: region I (England, Ireland, Belgium, and the Netherlands), region II (France, Italy, and Spain), region III (Germany, Switzerland, Denmark, Sweden, and Norway), and region IV (Czechoslovakia, Poland, Lithuania, Latvia, Estonia, and Russia).

In this study, the grain yield ratio for Europe was obtained by arithmetic averaging the grain yield ratios of the above regions. As the ratios are in 50-year units, the common logarithm of the data points was taken, linearly interpolated, and then anti-logged back, to create an annual time series.

The second parameter to represent agricultural production is the estimated annual values of agricultural production for Europe and China, which is calculated by the following steps:

step 1, linearly detrend population size and cereal price; step 2, convert the detrended population size and cereal price into positive numbers by adding positive integers; step 3: divide the "positive" population size by the "positive" cereal price; and step 4, smooth the resulting figure by 40-year Butterworth low pass filter.

NH 50-Year Fatality Index. It is calculated by the following steps: step 1, calculate the 50-year average fatalities per war (say A.D.1400-1449) from the known warfare fatalities record* and step 2, the average fatalities per war times the total number of war in that period to get the index.

Theoretical Issues

Cycle Theories. Many social scientists have noted cycles of social order and disorder in history, in which downturn periods were characterized by inflation, a fall in real wages, political crisis, war, and state breakdown (25, 26). There are many theories and hypotheses that attempt to explain this type of crisis and the dynamics behind the cyclical pattern of history, including mainly the monetarist, price, Malthusian, Marxist, neoclassical-economic, agrarian, environmental, and historical models. We ask: what caused periods of unusually frequent occurrences of these calamities simultaneously in widely separated regions of the world and can also explain how variations in this frequency follows a cyclical pattern through history? These eight models explain much about the causes of this set of calamities in this period, but none suffices to answer the questions posed above (27). For example, one may easily find significant correlations between social stability and other factors (agrarian, economic and population) in our analysis, that also support the monetarist, price, Malthusian, neoclassical-economic and agrarian models. However, those models cannot explain parallel occurrence of these crises in widely separated regions nor predict the timing of such crises. The agrarian and environmental models are similar to our model. But because there were no high-resolution paleo-temperature reconstructions at the time these models were proposed, and the explanations in these models lack population feedback, the models are not entirely convincing. Fischer (27) indicates that the final explanation for the cyclic phenomenon at hand requires another approach that combines their strengths and corrects their weaknesses and suggested that such an explanation should integrate ecological, demographic, social, monetarist, and economic factors.

A key component of our finding is that the cycles in two widely separated regions with different cultural, social, political, and religious systems had a synchronistic social reaction to climate change. For instance, the succession of the Medieval Order, Late Medieval Crisis, Renaissance, General Crisis, Enlightenment, and the Age of Revolution in Europe corresponds synchronously to the succession of the Northern Song Harmony, Southern Song and Yuan Collapses, Early Ming Harmony, Ming Collapse, Kang-Qien Harmony, and Lotus and Taiping rebellions in China (see SI Figs. 5B and 6B). The Ottoman Empire also experienced a similar cycle over the same long time span (27). Such a phenomenon was attributed to the societal tensions driven by price rises or excessive population growth (26-28). They were right if we do not look at the changes of climate and agriculture production. But, our study shows that price is a dependent variable that cannot generate the cycles by itself. Population growth in the long run is limited by land carrying capacity, at least in the preindustrial era. And food resources are in turn influenced by the climate. The long-term cyclic histories observed in those different regions were not autonomous. They in some significant part originated from a basic and deep-seated driver, climate change. Theories for the cyclic behavior based on price changes or population growth cannot explain why the war-peace and population cycles in different parts of the world, which were in different stages of civilization, culture, and resource endowment, occurred synchronously. These theories also cannot account for the timing of the long-term socio-economic cycles. Climate is the only plausible independent variable in the biophysical system shared by different parts of the world. Recently, research on the Pacific Basin also shows that cycles of societal disruptions in the last millennium are related to climate change (29). Therefore, our study that integrates human ecological, economic, demographic, and war dimensions successfully and quantitatively explains the synchronous war-peace and population cycles seen in widely separated regions and effectively predicts the turning points of long-term historical developments at global and continental scales in the past several centuries.

Demographic Theories. Malthus (30) assumes land carrying capacity to be essentially constant or possible monotonically increasing (arithmetic increase) and population increase is in exponential growth manner. Positive checks (i.e., famines, epidemics, and wars) and the associated population collapses occur when population growth overshoots the level of livelihood resources. Neo-Malthusians added some factors that influenced agricultural production and population growth upon the basic conception of Malthus.

Boserup (31, 32) argues that through the challenges to society which it posed, population growth often actually worked as the major dynamic engine of agricultural change, stimulating, in particular, the adoption of improvements in land use and technology. Moreover, this basically anti-Malthusian perspective can be further developed to argue that, other things being equal, the larger a population is, the larger will be the number of farmers, and therefore the greater will be the chance that someone will discover a new and more productive way of cultivating the available supply of land. In short, land carrying capacity increases with population growth, and that population growth will never exceed subsistence level.

The evidence of Malthus came from the individual cases of Europe in the 17th, 18th, and 19th centuries when human still did not have knowledge about climate change and technological transfer was very slow. Boserup's evidence was from a short period in several specific regions after the green revolution when technology transfer was much faster than the preindustrial era. Different periods and temporal scale on the research, of course, gained different conclusions. According to our research findings, Boserup's conception is only valid in very long-term (i.e., millennial scale) if we look the total trend of agricultural production. However, such intensive cultivation at the decadal and centennial scales could not keep pace with the needs of a growing population during periods when the climate cooled in Europe and China, as can be seen in various European economic records and the evidence from this study.

Our study supports that population checks will come into operation as population exceeds subsistence level. However, this study has emphasized that, given the technological limitations of agricultural production during the time, long-term climatic changes would have significant impact on food supplies, and that the constant or possible monotonically increasing of land carrying capacity (assumed by Malthus) was not true, at least in the preindustrial era. The upward trend of land carrying capacity is, in fact, characterized by shorter-term recurring oscillations (i.e., ascending wave pattern) in accordance with the alternation of cold and warm climate. This is the point not perceived by Malthus and many other contemporary scholars. Our study shows that when preindustrial populations climb up to the land carrying capacity, population pressure will be "autonomously" piled up, proceeding to the state of "hungry homeostasis" over time. When such a hungry homeostasis interacts with the climate-induced decline of land carrying capacity, it produces a demographic (or even socio-political) collapse through outbreaks of wars, famine, and epidemics. We accentuate that this mechanism determines the socio-economic and demographic cycles in preindustrial societies.

War Theories. The topic of war is a very popular topic for thousands of scholars, who applied their expertise to resolve this intractable problem. Sociologists, anthropologists, geographers, historians, political scientists, biologists, theologians, mathematicians, philosophers, and environmental scientists are only the most obvious of the categories (35). These scholars have developed their thought into the war theories. However, scholars have made scant progress on the problem even though members of all of the social sciences have addressed it (33). For example for the cause of war, historians have examined many factors such as governmental (mis)management or particular configurations of events. Many military scientists and political scientists have focused on the balance of power as a cause of war (34). Psychologists and biologists have discussed the nature of human aggressiveness (35), and Marxists have emphasized class struggle as a fundamental cause of war. It might not be a problem for historian to explain the cause of a specific case of war. Anthropologists study the armed conflicts caused by religious, cultural, and ideological differences. All of these theories have told us much about the causes of war. Some of these theories may explain certain wars, and some of the theories may even explain sizable classes of wars, but none of them can explain the temporal and spatial patterns of warfare presented in our research. Our research implies that relative food scarcity was a fundamental cause of war outbreaks according to our quantitative analysis on various physical and social components and in different geographical regions. Such a resource scarcity manifested itself in two causal pathways: a direct cause, in which resource-oriented wars erupted as most of the world's population still struggle to satisfy the lower levels of Maslow's hierarchy of needs (17); and an indirect cause, as constrained food resources and economic difficulties stemming from that intensified different social contradictions, that increased the likelihood of war outbreaks. The mechanical details of indirect cause still need to be studied.
